Google Design Challenge 2019

Prompt I Picked

Problem Statement

"A new school year is approaching and the orientation team is looking to you for some design expertise. Design an experience for new students to browse, search, and propose new student organizations".

This design exercise was conducted in Ann Arbor, Michigan and assumed orientation team is the University of Michigan's Campus for Student Involvement team who aim to

"Through meaningful involvement, create transformative experiences, a vibrant community, and lifelong memories for University of Michigan (U of M) students".

Thus the primary target audience would be students at the University of Michigan, especially those who have joined in the past one year.

Chose this prompt because of easy access to target audience. Image via GIPHY

How did I approach it?

My Overall Design Process

My first step was to make a plan for the week. How would I divide my time? Along the way, various questions came up from participants, data, and feedback sessions which led to going back and forth across this plan. With a week's timeline I prioritized user feedback and incorporated it at multiple points in the workflow.

Seeing mutiple survey's on the same prompt on student listservs, I chose to not to another survey to possibily biased data. Instead I focussed on stakeholder interviews, user interviews, iterating on wireframes, and gathering feedback on prototypes.



What did I find?

Methods used and insights discovered

To understand and empathize with the perspective of the target audience I conducted secondary research (on why student organizations are important in creating an inclusive learning community), put myself in their shoes (by googling ways to find student organizations on campus), 6 thirty minute semi structured interviews with students at the University of Michigan, and 1 stakeholder interview.

I created a modified version of a customer journey map inspired by Erika Hall's - Thinking in Triplicate.



Research questions

To narrow down the project focus I started brainstorming for potential research questions, each tied to one aspect of the problem statement (created post secondary research):

Browse - HOW DO STUDENTS LEARN ABOUT EVENTS AND CHOOSE ORGANIZATIONS OR EVENTS TO ATTEND?
Search - HOW DO STUDENTS SEARCH FOR STUDENT ORGANIZATIONS TO AFFILIATE WITH?
Propose - WHAT DO STUDENTS NOT LIKE ABOUT EXISTING ORGANIZATIONS?

insights and learnings from research

Browse - HOW DO STUDENTS LEARN ABOUT EVENTS AND CHOOSE ORGANIZATIONS OR EVENTS TO ATTEND?

The motivation behind looking for student organizations was rooted in tribalism, skill development , cultural familiarity, and networking through attending events hosted around campus.

Search - HOW DO STUDENTS SEARCH FOR STUDENT ORGANIZATIONS TO AFFILIATE WITH?

Users discovered student organizations (and events hosted) through social media, word of mouth, and occasionally emails.

Propose - WHAT DO STUDENTS NOT LIKE ABOUT EXISTING ORGANIZATIONS?

The common causes for frustration with student organizations was the lack of a "source of truth" which had updated information. U of M has 1600 student organizations live at the moment. Students did not know that what they might be looking for already exists!

What and how might I tackle this?

Ideation and Speculative Thinking for creating a process flow for possible solutions

Next step involved thinking about possible ways we could use the insights generated to create a feasible solution within the given deadline.

Wireframes Round One - Browsing, searching, and proposing student orgs.

Using my first set of wireframes I asked for feedback from the target audience from 3 participants through 15 minute feedback sessions. (For quick feedback I introduced the prompt and asked them walk me through what they understood from the wireframes.)

Research insight highlighting the close coupling of events and student organizations was seen in the feedback received here too. Search for student organizations was guided by the event they hosted. Hence, I explored an "Events tab" for too.

Wireframes Round One - Exploring an events page for organizations.

Participants also mentioned a need for a social element to the app. Especially features like members and easy identification of categories on the homepage. They compared it to group creation on Facebook Pages.

Wireframes Round Two - Refined versions for browsing, searching, and proposing student organizations.

Before creating high-fidelity prototypes I refined the profile page and homepage for organizations again. The key aim of this was to create a simpler object model and spread information between "organizations" and "feed" following the convention of a "homepage" and "news feeds page" on mobile apps today.

During a lecture during the week in an advanced UX research course, my class was tasked with spending a minute writting dowm words that came to our mind when we thought of Michigan. Maize-Blue and squirrels made it to the top of that list. This activity was aimed at helping us understand how we see ourselves compared how the rest of Michigan sees us - differing prespectives. Creating a visual system especially choosing a color palette and icon involved looking back at this activity and picking things the would match the target audience's mental model.
