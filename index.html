Google Design Challenge 2019

Prompt I Picked

Problem Statement

"A new school year is approaching and the orientation team is looking to you for some design expertise. Design an experience for new students to browse, search, and propose new student organizations".

This design exercise was conducted in Ann Arbor, Michigan and assumed orientation team is the University of Michigan's Campus for Student Involvement team who aim to

"Through meaningful involvement, create transformative experiences, a vibrant community, and lifelong memories for University of Michigan (U of M) students".

Thus the primary target audience would be students at the University of Michigan, especially those who have joined in the past one year.

How did I approach it?

My Overall Design Process

My first step was to make a plan for the week. How would I divide my time? Along the way, various questions came up from participants, data, and feedback sessions which led to going back and forth across this plan. With a week's timeline I prioritized user feedback and incorporated it at multiple points in the workflow.

Seeing mutiple surveys on this same prompt on student listservs at U of M, I chose to not do another survey due to the possibility of gathering biased data. Instead I focussed on stakeholder interviews, user interviews, iterating on wireframes, and gathering feedback on prototypes.



What did I find?

Highlights on Methods used and insights discovered


Empathize - Interviews, Journey map , and Secondary research

To understand and empathize with the perspective of the target audience I conducted secondary research (on why student organizations are important in creating an inclusive learning community), put myself in their shoes (by googling ways to find student organizations on campus), 5 thirty minute semi structured interviews with students (grad and undergrad) , 1 stakeholder interview, and 1 expert interview with a former student organization leader at the University of Michigan.

Recruitment was done by contacting students and orientation team members through emails.

insights and learnings from research

Artifacts created - Refined research questions and design principles

Browse - What motivates and achieves student involvement in organizations and events?

The motivation behind looking for student organizations was rooted in tribalism, skill development , cultural familiarity, and networking through attending events hosted around campus. Students discovered organizations through events and found out about the events through social media, word of mouth, and occasionally school emails.

DESIGN PRINCIPLE: Proposed solution should provide an inclusive space for students to search and be part of a diverse set of organizations as well as events. It should allow for flexibility and customization through conventional mediums without causing an information overload.

Search - How do students currently search for student organizations at U of M?

Students searched for organizations through social media like Facebook groups, newsletters on school related listservs, LinkedIn profiles of seniors , and word of mouth depending on their academic or cultural interests.
The orientation team introduces various student organizations on campus during the first week of orientation which happens in the first semester. Suprisingly, NONE of the participants recalled this as a source of information for campus involvement.

DESIGN PRINCIPLE: Students and orientation teams' prespectives on student organizations and its time of introduction might not overlap. Students requires a more need based and progressive access to this information as they get comfortable with campus culture. The solution should introduce student organizations at multiple points during a college life workflow.

Propose - What do students not like about student organizations and campus involvement activities?

The common causes for frustration with student organizations was the lack of a "source of truth" which had updated information. U of M has 1600 student organizations live at the moment. Students did not know that what they might be looking for already exists!

DESIGN PRINCIPLE: Proposed solution should prioritize reviews and the social aspect of community driven communication to aid in search for information.

Tweak with feedback

Using my first set of wireframes I asked for feedback from the target audience from 3 participants through 15 minute feedback sessions. (For quick feedback I introduced the prompt and asked them walk me through what they understood from the wireframes.)

Research insight highlighting the close coupling of events and student organizations was seen in the feedback received here too. Search for student organizations was guided by the event they hosted. Hence, I explored an "Events tab" for too.

Wireframes Round One - Exploring an events page for organizations.

Participants also mentioned a need for a social element to the app. Especially features like members and easy identification of categories on the homepage. They compared it to group creation on Facebook Pages.

Artifact created - Proposed features

1. A chatbot initiated mobile app which provides an easy way to browse, search, and propose student organizations on campus.

2. Students can filter by categories of interests, stay updated with articles on their feed, and find events around them. Organization specific pages provide details like feedback from past members, pictures from events, and easy way to follow plus join them.

3. The orientation team can have a centralized place to track organizations and events on campus (through CSI employee login). They can also easily approve proposals for new organizations.



Reflecting on my design and process

What could I do differently next time

Through this design exercise I tried to follow Google's design sprint phases. That helped ensure I had a deliverable at the end of the week and also appreciate the need for a team! As I was creating the prototype a bunch of questions came up and some of them required more research. If I could go back in time I would introduce more methods to collect user input and create a paper prototype.

A critical aspect of the proposed solution requires questioning scalability for a campus as big as U of M. A few other questions that can determine the value this solution brings to the proposed prompt are: Will the number of categories increase overtime? What is the impact of feedback received from students? Will having a version of the solution for the orientation team discourage students to give honest feedback?

The proposed solution is right now limited in terms of the conversational design of the chatbots. But using Google Dialogflow intents and categories could be easily prototyped to test with users.
